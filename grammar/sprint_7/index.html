1. Metaphor:

A metaphor is a figure of speech that directly compares two unrelated things, suggesting a similarity between them. It does not use “like” or “as” to make the comparison. Instead, it asserts that one thing is another thing.

Example: “Her voice is velvet.” In this metaphor, the person’s voice is compared to velvet, implying that it is smooth, rich, and pleasant.

2. Simile:

A simile is a figure of speech that also compares two unrelated things, but it uses “like” or “as” to make the comparison. It explicitly states that one thing is similar to another thing.

Example: “Her smile was like sunshine.” This simile compares the brightness and warmth of the person’s smile to the qualities of sunshine.

3. Figures of Speech:

Figures of speech are creative and rhetorical devices that add richness and depth to language. They go beyond literal meanings and enhance the expression of ideas.

Example 1: Personification - “The flowers danced in the breeze.” Personification attributes human characteristics (dancing) to non-human objects (flowers), making the description more vivid and engaging.

Example 2: Hyperbole - “I’ve told you a million times!” Hyperbole is an exaggerated statement that is not meant to be taken literally. It emphasizes a point or creates a strong effect.

Example 3: Alliteration - “Peter Piper picked a peck of pickled peppers.” Alliteration is the repetition of consonant sounds at the beginning of words in close proximity. It creates a musical and memorable effect.

Example 4: Oxymoron - “Bittersweet” or “Jumbo shrimp.” An oxymoron combines contradictory terms to create a unique meaning or impression.

Example 5: Onomatopoeia - “Buzz,” “hiss,” “crack.” Onomatopoeia refers to words that imitate or suggest the sounds they describe, adding sensory detail to the writing.

These figures of speech and literary devices add creativity, imagery, and emphasis to written and spoken language, making them powerful tools for communication and expression.

Omission of articles

Occasionally, articles are omitted altogether before certain nouns. In these cases, the article is implied but not actually present. This implied article is sometimes called a “zero article.” Often, the article is omitted before a noun that refers to an abstract idea. Look at the following examples:

Incorrect : Let’s go out for a dinner tonight.

Correct : Let’s go out for dinner tonight.

Incorrect : The creativity is a valuable quality in children.

Correct : Creativity is a valuable quality in children.

Tip: The words for many languages and nationalities are not preceded by an article.

Incorrect : I studied the French in high school for four years.

Correct : I studied French in high school for four years.

Tip: Sports and academic subjects do not require articles.

Incorrect : I like to play the baseball.

Correct : I like to play baseball.

Incorrect : My sister was always good at the math.

Correct : My sister was always good at math.
